  • User Research

    Before I start a project, I'd like to understand the topic, the industry and the overall problem. The most effective way is to talk to the users and stakeholders of the project to collect important information for the design process.

  • Concept creation

    After identifying the needs, pains and possible touch points people have, the hands-on designing can begin. Initial ideas are outlined and brought into a suitable form like wireframes or storyboards.

  • Visual design

    If the concept is perceived as possible solution, I begin to shape and craft interactions and interfaces. I mainly use Sketch or Photoshop for bringing scribbles and wireframes into life, making them ready for the next step: prototyping.

  • Prototyping

    Prototypes are essential for the design process as they give a realistic feel of the final product. They make a concept alive and seizable. Regardless of the fidelity, prototypes enable us to perform product tests with actual users in order to validate the underlying idea.

  • Testing

    To find out whether the concept is viable and works as envisioned, the prototype can be tested in various usability tests so that learnings and insights can immediately enrich the next iteration.

How might we design an enriching environment for animals kept enclosed?

PaceBreaker is a concept for an auditory scavenger hunt designed for predators that are not kept in their natural habitat and is dedicated to reduce stereotypical behaviour. Keeping the animals mentally occupied with species-appropriate challenges is an essential aspect of positive animal welfare. Modern technology has turned out to have great potential in this mostly untapped area.
